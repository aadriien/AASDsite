AutisticallyASD: FAQs



Common Traits & Behaviors

The repetitive and familiar nature of stimming can often provide comfort during times of stress and overwhelm. It enables the individual to channel their energy into a controlled source, promoting focus and stability. But stimming does not only serve to soothe; it may perhaps reflect a present physical or emotional state, such as strong happiness, tiredness, etc. It can also be the case that a person stims for no specific reason at all! Regardless of the situation, so long as the stim is not injurious to the individual or to others, it is perfectly ordinary, healthy, and beautiful.

While some autistic people are okay with eye contact, many find the experience to be immensely uncomfortable and unpleasant, to the point of it being painful. Maintaining eye contact can feel intense and overwhelming, especially with the added pressure of social nuance that accompanies such intimacy. Put simply, it is vital to respect the choices and the needs of the individual; they are still fully capable of listening to you even if they are not consistently holding your gaze. There is nothing wrong or offensive about avoiding eye contact, and it is crucial that the boundaries of the individual are respected.

Society as a whole has been designed to suit the needs of a neurotypical population. This means that neurodivergent communities often feel out of place, which initiates lifelong struggles of internalized distress. Largely as a result of this, routines allow autistic people to make sense of and cope with the chaos of the unpredictable neurotypical world. Routines offer solace, reliability, and the opportunity for rest, given that there exists a reassuring comfort in their familiarity.

Meltdowns occur when a person's threshold of tolerance has been irrevocably passed. The neurotypical world tends to be laced with complications that make neurodivergent life difficult, such as intense sensory stimuli (e.g. loud sounds, strong smells, large crowds), confusing social norms, systemic ableism, etc. These stressors add up, and once they reach a certain point, the autistic individual in question quite literally cannot maintain composure for any longer. Meltdowns are incredibly painful and embarrassing for the individual to experience; it is beyond their control. Nobody should ever be punished or shamed for a meltdown.

Simply put, autistic folks find joy, peace, and excitement in their special interests. They instill a sense of happiness and safety. Practicing Good Allyship

When describing someone with a diagnosis of ASD, identity-first language ("autistic person") is, according to the overwhelming majority of autistic people, preferred. Person-first language ("person with autism") has been popularized by the medical community and is commonly heard among allistic parents of autistic children, despite the fact that the autistic community has repeatedly spoken out against it. Yet each person has their own preference, so if possible, it is ideal to ask the individual what they prefer for themselves. Generally speaking, however, "autistic" is what works best in situations where you are unsure of individual preference, as well as in situations where a larger community is referenced.
