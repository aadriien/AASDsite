AutisticallyASD: A Guide to Authentic and Intersectional Neurodivergence


Definitions & Terminology




  • allistic:

    adjective, noun

    A person who is not autistic. It is possible that the individual in question could be neurotypical, though it could also be the case that they are neurodivergent. Phrased another way, an allistic person is someone who possesses any neurotype that does not involve autism.



  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) / autism / autistic:

    noun / noun / adjective

    A neurological condition, commonly considered to be a developmental disorder, that affects the ways in which an individual perceives and responds to their environment. Autism, or Autism Spectrum Disorder (abbreviated ASD), falls under the neurodivergent umbrella, owing to the fact that it is simply a difference in the way that the nervous system processes information. ASD occurs naturally, and it serves as a wonderful example of human biodiversity. ASD has been in existence long before it was labeled by researchers, and its unique traits have paved the way for major contributions to the development and progression of humanity. ASD can present in infinitely many diverse ways, and owing to the fact that society has been structured around the neurotypical experience, many autistic self-advocates consider autism to be a disability.



  • Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC):

    noun

    A means by which an individual can communicate without having to rely explicitly on verbal speech. More commonly referred to by its abbreviation of AAC, Augmentative and Alternative Communication can involve the use of an electronic device, a paper/chart, a whiteboard, etc. AAC is typically used by a nonspeaking neurodivergent population, but there are a number of verbal and partially verbal people who benefit from AAC. Such communication tools can be of particular help during times of meltdown and shutdown, as they enable the individual to convey thoughts, needs, and experiences at a time when the brain may not allow for fluid speech. AAC is just as valuable and authentic as any other form of communication, and nobody should ever lose access to, or be weaned off of, any communication tools that provide comfort and support self-articulation.



  • burnout:

    noun

    A powerful state of exhaustion experienced by an autistic person, which, if left untreated, can last weeks, months, or even years. A period of burnout can often look and feel similar to a shutdown, yet burnout involves a much lengthier, and typically more vicious, cycle of repeated distress. Common contributors to burnout can vary, but namely consist of instances where the individual pushes themselves excessively, whether it be at work/school, in social situations, in moments of sensory processing, etc. Burnout is exacerbated by a determination to keep going and to ignore one's needs (for example, neglecting to wear headphones or sunglasses in overwhelming settings for fear of being judged). Burnout is only ever alleviated when the individual implements a thorough period of rest, where they can address their needs and be free of any pressure to perform.



  • inspiration porn:

    noun

    A process by which the lives and actions of disabled people are glorified for the sake of uplifting the spirits of non-disabled people. It occurs when disabled people are condescendingly praised for engaging in various (oftentimes perfectly ordinary) activities. They may be labeled as "inspirational", "heroic", or any other number of descriptors, simply because their disability singles them out as being different from others around them. Common examples include but are not limited to: taking a disabled person to prom and very publicly sharing the news to others, posting videos online of a Deaf or Hard of Hearing person using hearing aids for the first time, viewing photos of people with mobility impairments engaging in athletics for the sake of giving oneself a motivational boost, etc. In short, if the idea of a disabled person participating in an activity gives you a "feel-good" pick-me-up, then you are likely perpetuating inspiration porn. Yet inspiration porn is incredibly ableist, as it dehumanizes the individual by suggesting that the mere existence of their disability renders them an object of inspiration. It allows non-disabled people to feel better about themselves becauses it reinforces their illusion of disability being some bad or inferior obstacle to overcome.



  • meltdown:

    noun

    An often explosive loss of control that is experienced by someone who has surpassed their threshold of tolerance. Some visible displays of an autistic meltdown include, but are not limited to, crying, screaming, injuring oneself/others, covering one's ears/eyes, and stimming, though each meltdown is specific to the individual. Meltdowns are extremely agonizing for the individual to experience, and are often accompanied by later feelings of shame, regret, tiredness, etc. They can be triggered by sensory overwhelm, emotional and social overwhelm, miscommunication, change in routine, and more. Meltdowns are completely involuntary, and always unwanted. Nobody should ever be punished for their meltdowns.



  • neurodivergence / neurodivergent:

    noun / adjective

    A variation in the way that an individual's nervous system (most notably their brain) functions. Someone who is neurodivergent has a neurology that operates differently from that which is found in the majority of the population. Different people have different perceptions of what this term entails, but generally, neurodivergence is considered to include conditions such as, but not necessarily limited to, Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), Tourette Syndrome (TS), Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD), dyslexia, dyscalculia, dyspraxia, and epilepsy. There exists debate as to whether certain mental health diagnoses should be classified under the umbrella of neurodivergence.



  • neurodiversity / neurodiverse:

    noun / adjective

    The collection of diverse neurological experiences. Neurodiversity exists in a group where some people are neurotypical and others are neurodivergent. It represents the many varied ways in which different individuals in a community can perceive and interact with the world. Neurodiversity is important for biodiversity, and it keeps society healthy and flourishing. There is no one neurotype that is superior to others.



  • neurotypical:

    adjective, noun

    A person whose neurology is most commonly found in the majority of individuals in the human population. This means that a neurotypical person's nervous system perceives and responds to the environment in a way that is similar to what is found in most other individuals. Because neurotypicals are expected to constitute the majority, our society is nearly always tailored to the neurotypical experience, which can leave little room for neurodivergent communities to find comfort and achieve success.



  • shutdown:

    noun

    A kind of internalized meltdown. The triggers of a shutdown are often the same as those of a meltdown, though shutdowns are usually harder for an outsider to notice. Some visible displays of an autistic shutdown include, but are not limited to, crying, spacing out, becoming very still, becoming unresponsive or otherwise delayed, and losing the ability to speak, though, like meltdowns, shutdowns are specific to the individual. Many compare shutdowns to an autopilot that takes over when the body and brain cannot push themselves any further. These internalized experiences are more common in adults, and they are just as emotionally and physically taxing to endure as a meltdown.



  • special interest:

    noun

    A book, TV show, movie, topic, concept, organism, etc. that brings an autistic individual joy, comfort, and amusement. Many autistic people feel very passionate about their special interests, and they can thus spend lengthy periods of time learning about, and subsequently communicating about, whichever topics attract their attention. Some people retain their special interests for years, while others may find new passions over the course of days. An individual can have multiple, few, or even no special interests.



  • stim / stimming:

    noun / verb

    A self-stimulatory behavior that is often performed repetitively. Stimming is done in a number of ways. It can involve interacting with one's body, and it can also involve interacting with fidget toys. Certain stims, such as rocking back and forth, flapping one's hands, making sound, spinning in circles, etc. are more visible. Other stims, such as bouncing one's leg, rubbing a soft object, twirling one's hair, clearing one's throat, viewing a colorful object, etc. are less visible, and therefore are less frequently noticed. Many neurotypicals engage in some small degree of stimming, whereas neurodivergent individuals tend to stim more frequently and more visibly. Stimming is natural and beautiful.
